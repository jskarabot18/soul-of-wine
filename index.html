The Soul of Wine

Terroir shapes what grapes can become. History, ambition, and culture determine what a region chooses to be.

59 regions · 6 identity clusters · ARI = −0.001
Abstract
The map is not the soul

This study asks whether the cultural identity of a wine region can be predicted from its physical terroir. We classify 59 wine regions from 16 countries along two independent axes: a terroir classification derived from TF-IDF vectorisation of climate, soil, and topographic profiles, and an identity classification derived from expert-scored dimensional ratings of anthropological character.

The result is unambiguous. The Adjusted Rand Index between the two clustering solutions is −0.001 — effectively zero. Knowing a region's terroir cluster provides no information about its identity cluster. The two systems are statistically independent.

The six identity clusters that emerge — Old World Interior, Outward Ease, New World Reinvention, Old World Exterior, Against the Odds, and The Moderates — reveal a typology of wine region character that transcends geography. Regions are grouped not by what the land gives them but by what they have chosen to make of it.

Key Findings
Three discoveries
1

Terroir shapes identity, but history modulates the signal

Physical geography creates the conditions for winemaking, but historical depth — time, tradition, institutional memory — is the dimension that most strongly differentiates identity clusters. The land sets constraints; the culture interprets them.

2

The type of terroir predicts the type of identity

Harsh, marginal terroir tends to produce identities of struggle and endurance. Generous terroir tends to produce identities of ease and pleasure. The relationship is real — but it is a tendency, not a law, and the exceptions are as revealing as the pattern.

3

Where terroir's constraints are weakest, human ambition fills the void

New World regions with favourable growing conditions show the highest winemaker ambition scores. When the land does not impose an identity, the winemaker constructs one. Ambition is the mirror image of terroir determinism.

Methodology
How the study works
Layer 1

Identity Descriptions

280–320 word anthropological narrative per region. Five dimensions: core metaphor, relationship to time, relationship to the outside world, the winemaker's role, distinctiveness.

Layer 2

Terroir Profiles

Six factual fields: climate, soils, principal varieties, winemaking, production structure, historical position. TF-IDF vectorised independently.

D-Scores

Identity Dimensions

Six expert-scored axes (−2 to +2): Interiority, Struggle, Tradition, Individuality, Urgency, Earthliness. Derived from Layer 1 descriptions by SME.

Pipeline

K-Means Clustering

D-scores standardised, PCA-reduced, k=6 K-means. Terroir clustered separately. ARI tests independence between the two solutions.
